Part I - Postmortem

A few months ago I wrote up a fairly lengthy post-mortem of Uncharted Worlds; where it succeeded, where it failed, and so forth. I figured I should repost a condensed version here as a foundation to the changes I‚Äôll hopefully be writing about in the coming weeks. A more complete/rambling version of the postmortem can be found here.

What Worked (What I‚Äôll be keeping or pushing further)

  • Archetypes: Constructing a custom ‚Äúplaybook‚Äù out of an Origin and two Careers ended up working super well, and is probably the crowning achievement of UW. Definitely keeping this almost unchanged.

  • Stat-neutral Moves: This a contentious one. Most of the situational Moves were rarely used and took up too much mental bandwidth, and the neutral Move (Face Adversity) did almost all the heavy lifting. Will push this further, drastically cutting down to just ‚Äúcore‚Äù Move while improving their guidance and usability. Moving the situational Moves out of the GM‚Äôs mental bandwidth.

  • One-roll combat resolution/no clocks: Another contentious one. Each triggered Move would act as a branching point in the narrative, rather than an increment towards a resolution. I‚Äôll be keeping this mostly intact, though I have some designs for the core Move (mentioned above) at will hopefully add a bit of strategic depth and decision-making.

  • Cramped Quarters: Another sleeper-hit, like Archetypes. This is probably the most exported mechanic I‚Äôve ever made; people have brought it over, whole-cloth or in spirit, to dozens of other games. Will push it a lot further. It might even be the vector I use to solve some of the problems I cover below.

What Didn‚Äôt Work (What will need to be redesigned/thrown out)

  • Economics: Wealth, resources, and acquisitions are an intrinsic part of the space opera/sci-fi experience. I tried too hard to hand-wave money, and it ended up causing more problems than it solved. Currency doesn‚Äôt need to be tracking individual credits, but it needs to have a reasonably sized denomination.

  • Factions: Not a complete failure, but certainly clunky and hit-or-miss at best. Factions were too restricted, too nebulous, and didn‚Äôt interact enough with the other mechanics. They‚Äôll need a solid re-design to make them feel more present and alive.

  • Advancement: Characters started too strong, with too many skills. Then they had nowhere to go, power-wise. Folks quickly ran out of skills they wanted. This also ties in with the Economy issue. Without visible monetary growth, ‚Äúxp‚Äù had to pick up the slack. One good piece of this was the ‚ÄòXp Trigger‚Äô, which rewarded acting within your career choices.

  • Excessive Genre Neutrality: Too much design space given to being wishy-washy, and trying to cover every permutation of sci-fi. Caused a lot of confusion, hemming and hawing during ‚Äúsession 0‚Äù and big tone clashes (people expecting Gundam, the Expanse, and Doctor Who out of the same game). While I don‚Äôt intend to create a fully-fleshed-out gonzo space opera setting, I need to at least pick a few core elements (tech level, FTL travel type, aliens, etc) and commit to them.

Whew. Gets a little easier to write every time I go through it. We learn from the past so that we can go on to make exciting new mistakes.

I‚Äôd be happy to unpack any of those topics further, if anyone has questions. Otherwise I‚Äôll try to post design updates in this thread every so often.

Part II - Mission Statement

While I‚Äôm sorely tempted to dive right in, rip out the mechanical guts of UW, and start tinkering, I think it would be more useful to both me and the project to take a step back and define UW2‚Äôs mission statement (as corporate-buzzword-y as that is). What am I trying to accomplish. What experience am I trying to evoke. By defining a clear goal for the game, I can subsequently use it as a reminder or point of comparison. I need to consciously refer back, and see if it encourages the players to achieve the goal of the game, or if it runs counter to the spirit of the game.

UW‚Äôs goal was nebulous. It was more-or-less ‚ÄúCommon Space Opera Tropes: The Game‚Äù. Early designs were all about that ‚Äúdo jobs to escape debt‚Äù kinda thing, but I never followed through on that goal. (I spent a long time flagellating myself about taking the middle-of-the-road coward‚Äôs way out and going as generic as possible, I won‚Äôt rehash it all here).

So this is UW2‚Äôs Goal going forward:

Find your independence out among the stars.

I‚Äôve thought long and hard about what aspects of UW that I liked most. At the end of the day, I have to like what I‚Äôm making, or I‚Äôll lose my passion for it. This goal sums up that preferred loop, that core element from which all others branch. Allow me to break it down:

  • FIND your independence out among the stars. Previous drafts of this goal had ‚ÄúFight for‚Äù or ‚ÄúClaim‚Äù. But I realize that part of this has to be a journey of self-discovery. You have to discover just how you will achieve your goal. I don‚Äôt want to curtail that freedom to choose your own methods, from combat, to diplomacy, to discovery, to exploration, to commerce. It‚Äôs YOUR stellar path to independence.

  • Find your INDEPENDENCE out among the stars. The balancing act between civilization and freedom will be one of the core conflicts of UW2. The heroes may take on contracts or exchange favors with all kinds of factions, but only in order to ensure their continued independence from any of them. The threat of being locked in chains (metaphorically or physically) should always be at the back of the characters‚Äô minds. The three most important loyalties are to your ship, your crew, and yourself.

  • Find your independence OUT AMONG THE STARS. There needs to be a focus on ‚Äúmoving on‚Äù, continually travelling from planet to planet, from adventure to adventure. Getting bogged down in the same place for too long changes the pace and tone of a game. Strong incentive towards ‚Äúknocking the dirt off your boots‚Äù and see what the universe has to offer.

So yeah, that‚Äôs the current design goal. It doesn‚Äôt overly restrict the genre (I do want to keep the flexible sandbox-y nature of UW), but it does help shape the expected play. If nothing else, it‚Äôll hopefully prevent me from designing dead-end or free-spinning systems.

I‚Äôm aware that ‚ÄúFind your independence out among the stars‚Äù is not exactly unique or distinctive compared to some sci-fi games where you swap minds with robots or travel through alternate timelines or what-have-you. That said, part of UW‚Äôs strength was that is was flexible, approachable, and unencumbered. I‚Äôm hoping this allows me to keep that aspect while still sharpening the focus somewhat. That said, I‚Äôm totally open to thoughts/opinions about it.

As a side note: @Deckard mentioned the Ironsworn Session-0 to establish the broad-strokes setting foundations, which is tech that I‚Äôll most certainly be incorporating (with Shawn Tomkin‚Äôs blessing).

  • Get Involved (Helping Move)

We‚Äôll just cover Face Adversity for now, and I‚Äôll cover Get Involved and Seek Opportunity at a later date.

In a way, this Move is 5 Moves, each with different consequences.
