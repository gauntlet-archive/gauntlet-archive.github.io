This is going to be incredibly dependent on play style.

I tend to play very early versions of classic D&D and the unspoken player goal is to reduce the amount of dice rolled because dice lead to uncontrolled risk, so for this sort of game a mechanic that attached character advancement and ability to intervene in the game world (via feats/special move) to die rolls would be largely counter productive. I would argue that it depends more on the group, or even the game master, than the actual game, unless there are written in the rules that you should roll X times per Y minutes.

For me, I mostly look at the numbers and determine a roll based on a predetermined scale. The dice sets the difficulty so I can decide the outcome as well as the dice. Things like clues, knowledge or lifting a stone seems weird to roll dice for. Instead, I just determine that you need X or more in skill/attribute value, and then you automatically succeed.

I also only want the players to roll dice whenever I can come up with a good consequence if they fail. That means that we only roll 4-5 times during a session in my games, outside of combat.
