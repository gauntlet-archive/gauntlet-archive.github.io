Hi! Yeah, it‚Äôs me from Story Games. I even have the same avatar.

Trouble I‚Äôm having with your premise is that the game master‚Äôs role is pretty traditional, and players will think that they are starting to treading too much on the game master‚Äôs territory when they start making up plots. I even had this experience with hardcore collaborative storytelling players. You need to set up the correct mindset for them to start contributing in the right way, but even that will diminish after a while if you don‚Äôt reinforce the mindset (of contributing) - mostly because of the outspoken game master role.

That said, I know of a couple of games that successfully managed to enforce, and keep, a high level of player influence.

The playtest documents for The Coyotes of Chicago is pretty unclear when it comes to how to play the game, but it‚Äôs almost like there everyone but one are game masters. The last person is the player, and the game masters have agendas and stories that they try to fit together with each other.

Kagematsu have a game master in form of a ronin that the players need to convince to save their town. Everything evolves around the ronin, but the players can suggest scenes to play out, based on a clear structure on their character sheet.

I burned myself out as a game master fifteen years ago, and almost abandoned roleplaying games, but then I discovered InSpectres, that presented a structure of play that made the game master mostly ask questions and come up with challenges, and then the players can do whatever to solve it. First half of the game is basically the players coming up with a lot of stuff, which will give you enough input to create a (silly) adventure for them to play.

I also think it‚Äôs easier to break a certain way of thinking by introducing specific moments in the game where the players should act in a certain way. For example, you could have specific roles in your game that introduces different elements. The jester just wants to create confusion between the players, the spider wants to create conflicts; you can even let these be gods that uses the ordinary character roles as token.

Or you can call for a specific scene type in your game where you take turns coming up with stuff that makes it harder for the player‚Äôs characters. ‚ÄúCome up with something about the player‚Äôs character on the right, that will make his/her life [xxx] (depending on the style of the game). One possibility is to ‚Äúoutsource‚Äù or ‚Äúexternalise‚Äù your distributed GM roles.

What I mean by this is for you to make the roles physical.

If you have five different roles that you require your players to inhabit as GMs, make five distinct tokens. Those tokens get physically handed out to the players.

The presence of the physical token becomes a mnemonic device, reminding all of the players that they‚Äôre meant to be doing whatever it is their tokens require them to do.

In addition to the tokens, you need to systematise the roles. Turn them into ‚Äúmoves‚Äù. ‚ÄòIf THIS happens, do THE FOLLOWING ACTION.‚Äô

And, you need to systematise passing the tokens from one player to the next. Among the lines of, ‚ÄòWhen this turn is over. Hand your token or tokens over to the next player, going clockwise.‚Äô

With everything systematised, and with external tokens, it becomes easy for the group to know what‚Äôs expected of each individual, at what point in the game, and for what purpose.

While I haven‚Äôt done this in gameplay, I‚Äôve done it in brainstorming sessions with groups. I have each person make a newspaper hat. We all have a bundle of different coloured slips of paper. Each colour represents a specific thinking skill to use in the brainstorm. (Blue=explorer, yellow=artist, green=judge, red=warrior ‚Äì these come from Roger von Oech‚Äôs CREATIVE WHACK PACK.) When we‚Äôre exploring the issue, we all pop a blue slip of paper into our hat rim.
