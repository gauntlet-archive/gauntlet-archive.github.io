A third way would be to set the stage by running the first foray as it‚Äôs own thing, and then shift over to resolving a week‚Äôs worth of exploration at a time.

If we decide we‚Äôre going to resolve each foray, I would ask them to Set Out, with the destination of ‚Äúfinding ancient ruins,‚Äù and give them some choices of directions to go (‚ÄúYou could head upriver toward the mountains, or into the rolling grasslands that stretch out to west, or cross the river and checkout the floodplain‚Äù), or just have them point at the map. To resolve the forays a week at a time, you could run it as the downtime move Work on a Project, with the project being ‚Äúfind the ancient ruins,‚Äù or call for a single Find Answers roll that sums up their explorations for the week.

If they‚Äôre just searching for anything of interest, without any rumors or knowledge of specific objectives, I would lean toward running one foray at a time, and just generate a random discovery from scratch or roll one off a pre-generated list.

Ah, apologies for not understanding you properly!

That stuff will be directly addressed in Overland & Underworld. My basic thinking at this point is:

  • In addition to the usual tags (safety, terrain type, etc.), assign each region a size tag (small, medium, expansive, vast) with some guideline to define each ‚Äì probably # of days it takes to cross the widest point on foot. So maybe small 1 day/medium 2-3 days/expansive 4-6 days/vast 7+ days.

  • Each size tag will have an associated ‚Äúfeature die‚Äù roll to determine the number of indigenous creatures, hazards, and discoveries, the same way that dungeon size determines # of areas. Let‚Äôs say the rolls are small 1d4+1/medium 1d6+2/expansive 1d8+3/vast 1d10+4.

  • To prep a region, you first roll the feature die for each category of creature, hazard, and discovery to determine how many of each type are present.

  • For each category, you roll up each of the different things on the existing tables. Given how I‚Äôm approaching dungeons, it makes sense to write up a set of tables based on specific terrain types, but for now you could just use the current general ones.

  • For each immobile hazard and discovery, you decide where it is on the map, or you leave it for the PCs to find wherever they happen to explore.

What do you think? See any problems with this idea? If you prep this way before you create incident tables, your prep could inform what you put on those tables.
