Let‚Äôs build on this ogre example, assuming our PC is relatively inexperienced.

This sum represents all the money you‚Äôve earned so far. Without it you won‚Äôt be able to afford an inn, let alone repairs to your armor and weapons. What do you do?

The goal here is to make it mechanically unlikely that the fighter will be able to take on and defeat the ogre without just making a pronouncement to that effect. I think Dungeon World can do this but it‚Äôs a bit tricky (part of this has to do with how the hack and slash move is written).

Let‚Äôs imagine our fighter has the following stats:

STR 16 (+2)
INT  9 (+0)
WIS 12 (+0)
DEX 13 (+1)
CON 15 (+1)
CHA  8 (-1)

So when this fighter rolls hack and slash they have a 42% chance of a 10+ (they deal damage without taking any), a 42% chance of a 7-9 (they deal damage and receive an attack in response), and a 16% chance of a 6- and something bad happening. In the ogre example, the DM‚Äôs best move is probably to add fictional requirements to be able to attack (e.g. roll defy danger with dexterity to get inside the ogre‚Äôs reach) or consequences to the ogre‚Äôs attacks (e.g. they don‚Äôt just hurt, they knock the fighter down, knock his breath out, terrify him, etc.).

The reason the latter aren‚Äôt as effective is that no matter how devastating the ogre‚Äôs counterattacks are there‚Äôs a 42% chance that they won‚Äôt happen (independent of how powerful the ogre is). The probability of consecutive 10+ results is reduced (~16% for back-to-back 10+), but if our hypothetical fighter can get an additional +1 bonus the probabilities become 58% for one 10+ result, 34% for two, and so on.

By contrast, if this fighter tries to bargain or trick the ogre (which might make more sense fictionally) they are rolling their worst stat (CHA) and have only an 8% chance of a 10+ and 42% chance of a 7-9. The DM doesn‚Äôt have an easy way to give them mechanical bonuses to the roll (or to reduce a difficulty) even though it targets one of the ogre‚Äôs weaknesses.
