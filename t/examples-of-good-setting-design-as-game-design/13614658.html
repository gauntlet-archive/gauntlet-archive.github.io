Ok, a few examples of setting design as game design:

  • In-world factions with niche protection such as Vampire clans in VTM with special clan specific powers, or Cults in RuneQuest that grant special role-relevant magic. Compare to classes, which are purely game mechanical constructs.

  • Reality rules. A sword is a sword in any setting, but what magic is available and how it works is part of the setting, but that can be whatever you like and affects gameplay a lot, so it‚Äôs both setting design and game design. The same goes for superpowers, psi, etc but also the technology in SF games.

  • Sources of Conflict. What‚Äôs the core activity for the characters? Who are the factions, allies, enemies, rivalries and activities for the characters? These are really important in generating action which feeds directly into what happens at the table. An example would be having an actual in world reason for having dungeons everywhere.
